§ 02

Promulgation & Adoption

The El Paso County Board of Commissioners approved this plan by resolution on January 13, 2026, and the City of Colorado Springs Mayor approved it by signature on January 16, 2026.

This plan provides planning and program guidance for activating and implementing the Emergency Operations Plan, supporting plans, guidelines, and processes — ensuring the organization can conduct its essential missions and functions under all hazards, threats, and conditions.

The plan is developed in accordance with the National Response Framework (NRF), Comprehensive Preparedness Guide 101 (CPG 101), and State of Colorado Department of Homeland Security and Emergency Management (DHSEM) guidance. It supersedes the previously issued Pikes Peak Regional EOP dated February 2, 2021 and is effective January 16, 2026.

§ 03

Executive Summary

PPROEM is a joint office of the City of Colorado Springs and El Paso County, established in January 2019 through an Intergovernmental Agreement. It is the intent of this plan to maintain an inclusive — rather than exclusive — relationship with other municipalities, towns, and special districts in El Paso County.

As outlined in this plan, PPROEM coordinates regional response during emergencies and disasters. The EOP establishes roles, responsibilities, and authorities for local, regional, state, and federal agencies, and ensures that operations align with the National Incident Management System (NIMS) and the National Response Framework (NRF).

The plan supports a flexible, all-hazards approach — enabling rapid, coordinated response to natural and human-caused emergencies. It emphasizes the importance of local decision-making authority while outlining mechanisms for requesting and integrating supplemental state and federal resources when local capabilities are exceeded.

The plan identifies common regional hazards, describes emergency management concepts of operations, and delineates the responsibilities of participating agencies through Emergency Support Functions (ESFs) and Emergency Coordination Center (ECC) sections. It incorporates considerations for vulnerable populations, individuals with access and functional needs, and the whole community to ensure inclusive, equitable response and recovery efforts.

§ 04

The Situation

PPROEM was created from an integration of the City of Colorado Springs Office of Emergency Management and the El Paso County Office of Emergency Management. The combined region encompasses 2,127 square miles and varies in terrain from high plains to the Rocky Mountains, culminating in 14,115-foot Pikes Peak.

The 2024 census places El Paso County's population at 752,772, making it Colorado's most populated county. The City of Colorado Springs is the second-largest municipality in Colorado, with a 2024 population of 493,554.

The region includes major regional facilities such as the United States Olympic Training Center, the United States Air Force Academy, Fort Carson, NORAD, Peterson Air Force Base, Schriever Air Force Base, and Cheyenne Mountain Air Force Base, along with multiple national non-governmental and corporate headquarters.

Regional scope

El Paso County contains 8 municipal jurisdictions, 25 fire departments and districts, 8 law enforcement agencies, over 200 special districts, 5 military installations, a university, a state college with three campuses, two private colleges, and 17 K-12 school districts plus multiple charter and private schools.

Capability Assessment

PPROEM's Continuous Improvement Program (CIP) is a structured, iterative process designed to build and assess emergency management capability over time. It links real-world performance and lessons learned to planning, training, and evaluation.

  1. Structured After-Action Process — captures lessons learned from real-world incidents and exercises through Evaluation Plans, Exercise Evaluation Guides (EEGs), feedback forms, hotwashes, surveys, interviews, and After-Action Reports (AARs).
  2. Action Tracking and Accountability — ensures identified improvements are documented, implemented, and monitored using Veoci updates, quarterly reports to senior leadership, and action status dashboards.
  3. Evaluation and Integration — measures effectiveness of implemented actions and integrates them into future planning, exercise design, and the Integrated Preparedness Plan (IPP).

The 2026 Hazard Mitigation Plan capability assessment evaluated four areas: planning and regulatory capabilities, administrative and technical capabilities, fiscal capabilities, and integration with other plans and programs.

§ 05

Purpose

This EOP is developed for use by PPROEM in support of county and municipal governments, and to support and coordinate with individual agencies to ensure prevention, preparedness, mitigation, response, and recovery for hazards affecting Colorado Springs, other municipalities, and unincorporated areas in El Paso County.

The EOP is intended to:

  • Identify the roles, responsibilities, and actions required of participating agencies, municipalities, departments, and enterprises in preparing for and responding to all-hazard emergencies and disasters.
  • Ensure a coordinated response by City, County, State, and Federal governments — saving lives, preventing injuries, and protecting property and the environment.
  • Provide a framework for decision-making, established policies, objectives, and approaches across jurisdictions.
  • Provide for the integration and coordination of volunteer agencies and private organizations.
  • Establish a whole-community framework for all plans developed and used by participating agencies, departments, and enterprises.
  • Guide operational decision-making — outlining procedures for activation, coordination, communication, resource management, and recovery.
  • Ensure local and regional compliance with the City of Colorado Springs Charter, El Paso County policies, and State and Federal guidelines.
§ 06

Scope

This EOP establishes the regulatory framework, legal authorities, and organizational structures that guide emergency management activities within El Paso County and the City of Colorado Springs. It defines the decision-making authorities at the local level and outlines how direction and control are maintained before, during, and after an emergency or disaster.

The EOP is based on an all-hazards approach that considers the region's common natural and human-caused hazards. It outlines how emergency operations begin with the jurisdiction having authority and how support is organized when an incident exceeds local capabilities. PPROEM provides coordination and assistance to local jurisdictions during significant incidents or when a routine emergency has the potential to escalate.

State assistance may be provided as a supplement to local and regional efforts, and will be requested by local agencies on behalf of El Paso County and/or the City of Colorado Springs when incident needs exceed regional capacity. Federal assistance may be requested through the State of Colorado in accordance with statutory authorities and established processes, as approved by the Governor.

§ 08

Planning Assumptions

The following assumptions apply to this plan:

  • Emergency management activities within the region will be conducted in accordance with NIMS.
  • Emergencies and disasters will be managed at the lowest jurisdictional level with the authority and capability to effectively respond.
  • Local authorities retain operational control and decision-making responsibility within their jurisdiction unless superseded by statute, legal delegation, or established agreements.
  • Mutual aid will be requested when needed and provided to the extent available.
  • Mitigation activities conducted before an incident will reduce the likelihood and severity of impacts.
  • Supporting plans, procedures, and checklists maintained by departments, agencies, and partner organizations will be kept current.
  • Incidents may occur with little or no warning and may escalate rapidly, requiring immediate coordinated response.
  • Resources may become limited, damaged, or overwhelmed during a major emergency or disaster.
  • Communication and information systems may be degraded or disrupted, requiring alternate methods and redundant systems.
  • The whole-community approach guides all emergency management activities — programs, services, and activities will be inclusive of individuals with disabilities and others with access and functional needs.
§ 09

Concept of Operations

Pikes Peak regional jurisdictions use NIMS and the Incident Command System (ICS) as the foundation for managing emergencies and disasters. These systems provide a standardized, flexible, and scalable structure for organizing response operations, coordinating resources, and supporting decision-making.

Emergency management activities across the region align with five mission areas — prevention, protection, mitigation, response, and recovery. Preparedness actions are conducted year-round and documented in the PPROEM Integrated Preparedness Plan (IPP).

Phases of Emergency Management

Preparedness serves as the foundation for all phases — the ongoing, proactive effort to ensure communities, organizations, and individuals are ready to respond and recover effectively.

  1. Prevention

    Actions to avoid or stop an incident from occurring — public health interventions, law enforcement and intelligence, code enforcement, and security measures.

  2. Protection

    Strategies to safeguard people, property, infrastructure, and the environment — physical security, public warning systems, and coordination to reduce exposure.

  3. Mitigation

    Long-term measures to minimize disaster impact — land use planning, building codes, flood control, wildfire fuel reduction, and infrastructure investments.

  4. Response

    Immediate, short-term action to save lives — ICS/ECC activation, search and rescue, sheltering, public information, and resource coordination.

  5. Recovery

    Restoration and improvement of community functionality — infrastructure restoration, economic recovery, mental health services, and after-action learning.

Multi-Agency Coordination (MAC) Group

The PPROEM MAC Group serves as a central mechanism for enhancing regional preparedness, coordination, and unified action. It is composed of representatives from key partner agencies and jurisdictions across the Pikes Peak Region and is activated to support large-scale or complex emergencies that require cross-agency collaboration — coordinating communication, resource sharing, policy and decision-making, problem solving, and information sharing.

Levels of Government

Local Government Management
The authority having jurisdiction maintains direction and control before, during, and after an emergency. The hierarchy depends on the type of declared state of emergency.
Incident Level Management
A local incident management system that incorporates the functions, principles, and components of NIMS is adopted and used. Common terminology and a flexible, expandable structure make this system suitable for multi-functional response.
Local Level Management
Local governments are responsible for overall coordination of support for emergency operations. The on-scene Incident Commander manages activities at the incident site; local government coordinates administrative and off-site functions. The ECC is activated when an incident threatens to exceed local capabilities, including mutual aid.
State Level Management
When an emergency overwhelms the resources and capability of a local jurisdiction, the Governor may exercise authority to deploy state resources. DHSEM implements the State's response, coordinating acquisition, prioritization, and distribution of state, federal, and private resources.
Federal Level Management
When an emergency exceeds state and local capabilities, federal support is provided consistent with the National Response Framework. FEMA, under the Department of Homeland Security, is the primary agency for coordinating federal disaster assistance.

Incident to Disaster & Declaration Process

Emergency operations typically begin locally under the authority having jurisdiction (AHJ). An incident becomes classified as a disaster when its scale, complexity, or impact exceeds the capability of local response agencies.

Upon a local disaster declaration and ECC activation, PPROEM assumes responsibility for regional support and coordination. In the Pikes Peak region, a disaster may be declared by the Board of County Commissioners for El Paso County or the Mayor for the City of Colorado Springs. The declaration triggers activation of the EOP and associated ESFs, authorizes resource requests beyond local capabilities, and enables the implementation of emergency authorities as provided by law.

Policy Groups

The PPROEM Director is the connection between the ECC and the City and County Policy Groups. During a disaster, the Policy Group may convene to provide strategic direction — establishing emergency management policies, drafting and supporting implementation of a disaster declaration, and making policy-level decisions such as evacuation orders or curfews.

§ 10

NIMS & Incident Command Structure

NIMS provides a consistent framework for all levels of government, NGOs, and the private sector to work together to prevent, protect against, mitigate, respond to, and recover from incidents. It establishes a common vocabulary, standardized organizational structures, and processes that enable stakeholders to coordinate effectively.

Key elements of NIMS include:

  • Modular organization — ICS and ECC structures expand or contract based on incident size, complexity, and hazard environment.
  • Unified command — multiple jurisdictions or agencies set priorities and approve joint objectives when no single entity has full authority.
  • Common terminology — standardized language ensures clear communication across agencies and hazards.
  • Integrated communications — connectivity, situational awareness, and information sharing across all levels of response.
  • Incident command — clear chain of authority for on-scene management while coordinating support from agencies, the ECC, and other resources.

Incident Types

NIMS/ICS uses a 5-level scale to categorize incident size, magnitude, and complexity — with Type 1 the most complex (catastrophic, requiring national resources) and Type 5 the most routine (handled by local on-scene resources). Approximately 95% of incidents are Type 5, 4, or 3.

§ 11

Emergency Coordination Center (ECC)

The ECC's organizational structure is based on NIMS principles, operating as a function-based system that is flexible and scalable to meet the region's needs. It is designed to facilitate coordination and communication among municipal, county, state, and other partner agencies.

PPROEM has identified seven critical functions of the ECC: Policy Group and ECC Leadership Coordination, Operational Coordination, Resource Management, Public Communication, Information Management, Community Support, and Transition to Recovery.

Information system

The ECC uses Veoci as its disaster management software for information management, resource mobilization, and records and documentation retention.

ECC Sections

Direction

Provides overall leadership, strategic guidance, and administrative support. The PPROEM Director serves as the primary liaison to the Policy Group; the ECC Manager oversees the ECC's daily operations; the ECC Deputy Manager supports continuity.

Resource

Manages acquisition, allocation, and accountability of personnel, equipment, supplies, and funding. Oversees procurement of emergency supplies, maintains detailed financial records, coordinates reporting for state and federal reimbursements, and provides funding guidance.

Coordination

Ensures effective collaboration among municipal, county, state, and partner agencies. Organized into the Infrastructure Group (public works, engineering, transportation, utilities, lifeline functions) and the Emergency Services Group (fire, law enforcement, and other emergency operations).

Information

Manages collection, analysis, and dissemination of information. Provides situation reports, planning support, and documentation; coordinates with communication partners and jurisdictional GIS staff to maintain situational awareness.

Community Services

Addresses the needs of the whole community, including vulnerable populations and individuals with access and functional needs. Coordinates mass care operations, social services support, and community assistance activities.

ECC Activation Levels

PPROEM On-Call — Daily Operations
PPROEM staff carries on-call phone and actively monitors response activity using radios, weather radar, CAD, and paging systems. The On-Call provides situational awareness to PPROEM Leadership.
PPROEM Monitoring & Coordination
Triggered when threat indicators are high — Red Flag Warning, Fire Weather Watch, significant planned event, snow event watch and warning, flood advisory/watch/warning. May include Everbridge notifications and direct coordination with City, County, and partner departments.
PPROEM Staff Activation
At least two PPROEM staff are actively engaged in support of an incident, remotely or in the ECC. Support includes information management, public information, coordination, and resource ordering.
ECC Activation
Opening of the PPROEM ECC at 3755 Mark Dabling Blvd. Includes Everbridge notifications to fill essential positions in needed sections (Direction, Information, Resources, Coordination, Community Services), notification of policy group members, and notification of partner departments.

Authority to Activate

The PPROEM ECC may be activated by the Emergency Management Director, PPROEM staff, the El Paso County Administrator or Assistant Administrator, the Board of County Commissioners, the Sheriff or Undersheriff, the Public Health Director, the Colorado Springs Chief of Staff or Deputy Chief of Staff, the Mayor, the Fire Chief, or the Police Chief — or their designees.

§ 13

Resource Management

During a disaster, personnel, equipment, supplies, and financial resources may be needed to protect life and property. Clarity, efficiency, and accountability in resource management are critical.

The resource management process follows a structured four-step cycle that ensures personnel, equipment, supplies, and facilities are identified and requested, acquired and mobilized, assigned and tracked, and demobilized in an organized manner.

The Four-Step Cycle

  1. Request

    Operational entities identify needs and request resources.

  2. Mobilize

    Ordering agencies acquire and mobilize the requested resource.

  3. Utilize

    Operational entities receive, distribute, and track resources.

  4. Demobilize

    Operational entity demobilizes unneeded resources.

§ 14

ECC Transition to Recovery

The transition from emergency response to recovery is a critical phase. The ECC facilitates this transition by coordinating with all activated ESFs, local jurisdictions, and supporting agencies — ensuring immediate response operations are safely concluded while recovery priorities are established.

This process includes identifying affected populations, assessing infrastructure and facility impacts, and coordinating the collection and dissemination of damage and needs assessment data to support timely recovery actions.

Recovery operations in the Pikes Peak region are guided by the PPROEM Recovery Plan, which outlines the framework for short-term and long-term recovery — restoration of critical services, infrastructure rehabilitation, and support for impacted individuals and communities. The ECC serves as the central hub for transitioning operational control, maintaining situational awareness, and communicating recovery priorities to all partners.
