Metro District Citizen Toolkit

Five letters. All the contacts. Plain language. No lawyer required. Everything you need to push for real data, ADA compliance, and district transparency. This is not legaly advice, seriously.

Most residents are in at least one metro district without knowing it. Work through these steps to find yours.

Step-by-step: Find your district
1
Go to elpasoco.com/assessor and search your address. Look for "Special District" entries in the property detail.
2
Try dola.colorado.gov/lgis/ — the Colorado Local Government Information System. Search El Paso County to see all registered special districts.
3
Use the cheetochopsticks jurisdiction finder — type your address, see every government layer touching your parcel.
4
File a CORA request with the EPC Assessor at asrweb@elpasoco.com asking which metro districts your parcel falls within. Response required within 3 business days.
5
Check your property tax bill. Special district mill levies appear as separate line items with the district name listed.
What to look for — and why it matters
▸
Mill levy — a property tax assessed each year. No EPC district publicly explains its rate, basis, or history. You are paying it regardless.
▸
Bond debt — secured against your property. Outstanding balances and maturity dates are not publicly posted by any audited EPC district.
▸
Service plan — required by C.R.S. §32-1-202. None of the 8 audited EPC district websites post theirs publicly.
▸
Annual audit — required by §32-1-106, due to the State Auditor by July 31. Not one audited district posts this publicly.

This letter asks the BOCC to require that military housing and population data used in district planning be based on real administrative records — not census estimates or survey samples — and that verified numbers be publicly available by January 2027.

Why this matters: Census and survey data are sample-based estimates. Real unit counts exist in Fort Carson administrative records, the EPC Assessor database, school district enrollment systems, and utility connection data. These are actual counts — not projections. Metro district bond sizes and mill levies should be based on actual numbers. So should decisions about who needs to be served.
The current gap: District service plans and bond documents cite market research firms and census projections to justify debt levels secured against your property. If an estimate is 15% high on a $90M bond, that is $13.5M of debt sized on a guess. The real data exists. We are not currently requiring its use.

Cascade Avenue, Colorado Springs, CO 80903 JackieAllred@elpasoco.com Re: Request for Policy Resolution — Use of Verified Administrative Data (Not Census Estimates) in Metro District Planning, Bond Justification, and Public Reporting — Published by January 31, 2027 Dear Chair Geitner and Members of the Board, My name is [YOUR NAME]. I am a resident of El Paso County at [YOUR ADDRESS]. I write about a data quality issue that affects every major decision made about El Paso County's 284 metropolitan districts — how much debt they issue, what mill levies they charge, and what services they claim to provide. THE PROBLEM — DECISIONS MADE ON SAMPLE DATA WHEN REAL DATA EXISTS Metropolitan district service plans, bond documents, and planning submissions routinely cite: -- U.S. Census Bureau estimates (American Community Survey — 5-year sample data) -- Third-party market research projections (Zonda Colorado, CBRE, CoStar) -- Regional housing surveys conducted on sample populations These are estimates. They are not counts. They are not verified. They are used to justify financial obligations — bonds, mill levies, and infrastructure commitments — that are secured against the property of actual residents. Real administrative data already exists and is available to the county at no cost: FORT CARSON / JBLK: Actual military household unit counts, family housing occupancy records, and personnel assignment data are maintained by the installation and available through intergovernmental agreement. This is not a survey. It is a roster. EPC ASSESSOR: Actual parcel counts, ownership records, improvement values, and tax unit data by district. An administrative record, not an estimate. SCHOOL DISTRICTS: Actual enrollment by school zone and feeder area. Real children. Real addresses. UTILITY PROVIDERS (Colorado Springs Utilities, Black Hills Energy, Fountain Valley Authority): Actual service connections and meter counts. Real units. Real addresses. PPRTA AND TRANSIT: Actual ridership and stop-level counts in military and suburban corridors. None of this requires a new study. It requires an intergovernmental data-sharing request and a willingness to use data that already exists. WHY THIS MATTERS FOR QUALITY DECISIONS When a metro district issues $90 million in bonds based on projected population growth — and that projection is a census estimate with a known margin of error — the bond is sized on a sample. If the sample is 15% high, the debt is $13.5 million larger than the actual population justifies. Residents pay that difference through higher mill levies or reduced services for longer than necessary. Data-driven decisions require actual data. Census and survey estimates are appropriate when administrative records do not exist. In El Paso County, they do exist. We simply are not requiring their use. THE ASK I respectfully request that the Board adopt a resolution requiring: 1. All EPC metro district service plan submissions, bond justification documents, and annual reports cite actual administrative data sources — EPC Assessor records, verified utility connection counts, school district enrollment figures, and intergovernmental data from Fort Carson — rather than census estimates or market research projections alone. 2. The BOCC initiate an intergovernmental data-sharing agreement with Fort Carson / JBLK to obtain verified military household unit counts for planning documents affecting districts in the Fountain Valley and southeastern EPC corridors. 3. The EPC Assessor publish, by January 31, 2027, a publicly accessible dashboard showing verified parcel counts, unit counts, and district boundaries for all 284 EPC metro districts — updated annually — so residents, planners, and officials make decisions from the same verified baseline. 4. Any bond submission for a new or expanded EPC metro district that relies on projected rather than verified population figures include a plain-language disclosure of that fact and its stated margin of error. This is not a request to stop using projections. It is a request to use real data first — and to make the gap between real data and projections visible to everyone who lives with the consequences. Respectfully, [YOUR FULL NAME] [YOUR ADDRESS] [YOUR PHONE / EMAIL]
