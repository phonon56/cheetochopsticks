A Dashboard · Twenty-One Years of Residential Water Accounts

What was produced,
and what cannot be reconciled.

Colorado Springs Utilities produced the four aggregate datasets the requester asked for — after initially stating that three of them were not tracked. The numbers show a utility roughly two-and-a-half times the size it was in 2005. They also show at least 26,000 accounts whose existence the openings-and-closings ledger cannot explain, and a single 2021 drop of nearly 38,000 active customers that no one has accounted for.

§ 01 · Sheet One

Active Residential Accounts, 2005–2026

The headline trend: steady growth from the mid-sixty-thousands through 2020, a cliff in 2021, and a rapid climb to a new high by 2025. The 2026 figure is year-to-date and partial.

The 2021 Cliff

152,537 to 114,898. A single-year drop of 37,639 accounts — roughly a quarter of the customer base. No regional event explains a loss this size; the likely cause is a data cleanup, system migration, or definitional change that was never disclosed in the production.

The Pre-2009 Plateau

Active accounts are essentially flat from 2005 to 2008 (68k to 68k), then growth resumes sharply in 2009. This is not what the opens and closes ledger shows — opens outpaced closes by about 2,000 every year in this window.

The Recovery

From 2022 forward, active accounts grow faster than opens minus closes would predict. By 2025 the active count sits 11,000 plus higher than the flow data justifies.

§ 02 · Sheets Two & Three

New Openings & Closings, in Parallel

Customer churn at CSU is remarkably stable. Across twenty-one years the utility has opened and closed roughly the same number of residential accounts each year, with a mild decline beginning around 2023.

Stability, Not Noise

Annual openings sit between 24,000 and 32,000 across the entire 21-year window. Annual closings sit between 21,000 and 38,000. Both series move slowly; neither shows a 2021 shock.

Net Growth is Real but Modest

Opens minus closes yields plus 64,592 net new accounts across 2005 to 2025. Active accounts grew by plus 99,398 in the same window. The 34,806 difference is the core reconciliation gap.

The 2023 Step-Down

Opens drop from about 28,000 to about 24,000 starting in 2023 without a matching drop in closes — a soft slowdown in new service worth flagging in future requests.

The Central Finding · Reconciliation

The data as produced cannot be reconciled against itself. Opens and closes predict one story; the active-account totals tell another. Over twenty-one years the two stories diverge by roughly the population of a small city — the openings-and-closings ledger explains only about 65 percent of the growth reported in the active-accounts sheet.

§ 03 · The Silent Variable

Year-Over-Year Active Change, versus Net Opens Minus Closes

If the three sheets described the same customer base under the same definitions, these two series would match. They do not.

Persistent, Not One-Off

The reconciliation gap is not isolated to 2021. It is consistently positive from 2012 forward, meaning more active accounts appear each year than the opens and closes math can justify — suggesting Sheet 1 and Sheets 2 and 3 are measured against different definitions or systems.

Pre-2012 Looks Different

In 2006 to 2008 active accounts fell while openings exceeded closings. Either CSU was retiring accounts outside the normal closed category, or the pre-2009 baseline uses a tighter definition of active.

2021 is the Outlier

The 2021 gap of negative 43,529 has the opposite sign of every year around it. This is the most probable signature of a one-time data cleanup, CIS migration, or retroactive redefinition of active — and it has never been disclosed.

§ 04 · Sheet Four

Near-Zero Usage Accounts, a Rising Curve

CSU's operational proxy for an inactive account — billed under one cubic foot of water six or more times in a calendar year — shows a fifteen-year decline followed by a reversal beginning in 2021. The count has nearly doubled since.

A Definition, Finally

This is the only sheet in the production that includes an operational definition of the metric — less than 1 CF billed, 6 or more times per year. The absence of comparable definitions on Sheets 1 through 3 is itself a transparency gap.

2005 is Missing. 2006 is Suspect.

2005 appears as "not available" with no explanation. 2006 shows 19 — so far outside the surrounding trend that it almost certainly represents a partial or incomplete measurement, not a true count.

Reversal Since 2021

The near-zero usage count fell steadily from 2008 through 2018, then climbed from 596 to 1,304 between 2018 and 2025 — a 119 percent increase. Worth a follow-up on what is driving it: vacancies, conservation success, economic stress, or seasonal-use properties.

§ 05 · Gaps in the Production

What the Workbook Does Not Say.

A complete production answers the requester's question and also documents its own provenance. This workbook does the first; it does not do the second.

Missing

No data run-date or cutoff disclosed

The workbook contains no header, no cover sheet, no metadata. "2026" could be through any date between January 1 and April 23. CORA productions of database extracts should disclose when the query was run.

Missing

No source system identified

Were these figures pulled from the Customer Information System, the rate-case workpapers, the Annual Operating and Financial Plan, or a combination? A re-run against a different source would yield different numbers.

Inconsistent

Sheet 1 does not reconcile to Sheets 2 and 3

Active-account year-over-year change exceeds net opens minus closes by 34,806 over the full series. Either a definitional difference exists across sheets or the data is drawn from different systems.

Inconsistent

2020 to 2021 drop of 37,639 unexplained

A single-year loss of roughly a quarter of the customer base with no corresponding rise in closings, no regional event, and no note in the production. Strongly suggests a CIS migration or retroactive reclassification.

Missing

Sheet 4: 2005 is marked not available

No explanation for the gap. Is the metric not computable pre-2006? Was it not tracked? Was the query simply not run that far back? The distinction matters for anyone using the data longitudinally.

Undefined

Sheet 4: 2006 count of 19

The 2006 value is roughly one fiftieth of the surrounding years. Almost certainly a partial-year or mid-migration artifact, but unlabeled as such. A production should flag known-incomplete cells, not leave the reader to infer them.

Undefined

"Active" is never defined

Sheet 4 defines its metric precisely. Sheets 1, 2, and 3 do not. The data-definition footnote should accompany each sheet: what is an "active" account, what does "opened" mean operationally, what counts as "closed."

Missing

No 2026 year-to-date cutoff label

All four sheets include 2026 figures, but nothing marks them as partial-year. Item 4 shows "not available" for 2026 (defensible, given the metric needs a full calendar year), but Items 1 through 3 mix partial and complete years without disclosure.

About This Dashboard

This dashboard was compiled from the XLSX workbook produced by Colorado Springs Utilities in response to CORA request W001936-041726, delivered via the GovQA portal on April 23, 2026. It is a reader's tool, not an audit: numbers shown are exactly as produced, and derived columns (Net Opens minus Closes; Year-over-Year change in Active; Reconciliation Gap) are simple arithmetic on the produced figures.

Where this dashboard describes data as "unexplained" or "unreconciled," it does not imply error or bad faith on the part of CSU — only that the production as delivered does not provide the definitional or source-system metadata needed for a requester to independently verify the three flow sheets against the active-account totals. Recommended follow-ups are enumerated in Section 5.
