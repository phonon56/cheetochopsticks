El Paso County has had net domestic out-migration every decade going back to the 1990s. The county's population grew only because births and international immigration outpaced the people leaving. That structural pattern is now weakening — births are declining and international migration dropped 55% nationally in 2024–2025.

Births are real counted records — but they don't directly create new households. A birth adds to the population of an existing household. What creates housing demand is net new household formation — which requires both a person and a separate address. That figure is not tracked in any real-time administrative system.
Migration — the number nobody actually counts
There is no government system that counts who moves into or out of El Paso County each year. Net migration is a Census Bureau calculation — total population change minus natural growth — not a direct measurement. Nobody counts U-Hauls, change of address forms are not compiled by county, and driver's license transfers are not published.

The CSU utility account data — new accounts opened and closed per year — is the closest thing to a real count of people moving in and out that exists at the county level. It captures actual physical residency changes, not survey estimates.
The Census Bureau's migration estimate is calculated as a residual — total population change minus births plus deaths. It absorbs all measurement error from every other estimate. It is not measured independently.
The military distortion — unique to El Paso County
Fort Carson, Peterson Space Force Base, Schriever Space Force Base, and the Air Force Academy collectively represent one of the largest military concentrations in the US. Military personnel and their families cycle through El Paso County on 2–3 year rotation orders — creating a population churn that looks identical to civilian in/out migration in Census estimates but is structurally different.
Military personnel on-post at Fort Carson live in federal housing not tracked by the county assessor, pay no property tax, and generate no utility account with CSU. They are present in the population but invisible to nearly every administrative data system used for planning.
When military relocations slow — as happened in 2024 per market reports — apartment absorption spikes, school enrollment shifts, and retail demand changes. But no planning system predicted this because military rotation data is federal and not shared with the county.
Basic Allowance for Housing (BAH) paid to military families distorts rental market pricing — landlords price to BAH rates, pushing rents above what civilian incomes can support. When rotation orders change, that demand disappears without warning.
A FOIA request to the Department of the Army for Fort Carson annual on-post housing occupancy counts, and a similar request to Peterson/Schriever for off-post housing allowance recipients by ZIP code, would be the only way to account for this population in any real planning model.
