Compared to What

An honest look at wages, housing, and public investment in El Paso County and Denver County.

The Chamber publishes the wins. This dashboard publishes the comparison. Three charts, two counties, six years of federal and Zillow data — and one column still being assembled while public records requests work their way through. Methodology is at the bottom. Every number is sourced.

Headline finding · 2024
An average El Paso County worker earns $66,313. The income needed to buy a typical home in the Colorado Springs metro is $114,656. The wage covers 57.8% of what's required.

* Footnote on SelectTech and announced-vs-delivered jobs: Public reporting indicates SelectTech Services Corporation currently employs roughly 50 to 150 people total with annual revenue in the $17M–$28M range. The announced 150-job El Paso expansion would effectively double the company's existing workforce — meaning the announcement is a forecast tied to future federal contract wins, not the relocation of an existing operation. JGITC is performance-based: OEDIT issues credits only as jobs are created and maintained at or above the county average wage for a full year. If announced jobs don't fully materialize, the JGITC simply doesn't get paid. Other incentive layers (local cash, abatements, fee waivers) may flow earlier and don't all carry the same performance gates. Announced jobs are not delivered jobs — and per-job calculations from announcement-stage data should be read accordingly. The OEDIT Annual Reports include a "JGITC Tax Credit Certificates Issued" table that tracks delivered against approved; that comparison is the cleaner long-term measure and will be added in a future dashboard update.
Context on the cleared-workforce labor pool: The pool of Americans holding active security clearances declined from roughly 5.1 million in 2013 to about 4.2 million in recent reporting from the Office of the Director of National Intelligence (ODNI Annual Report on Security Clearance Determinations). Clearance processing times regressed in 2024, with top-secret investigations averaging 70 days longer than the prior year. Federal contractor industry surveys consistently identify cleared talent acquisition as a top operational concern. SelectTech's 150-job announcement plus ITS's 500-job announcement in the same week represent meaningful demand on a finite regional pool of cleared workers — which has implications for whether announced jobs can be filled at announced wages, or whether competition will push compensation upward.

Reading What the data is telling us

The Chamber & EDC's pitch to recruiting companies, in their own words, is that "Colorado Springs' lower wages gives it an edge over cities of similar size." That works as a recruiting line for companies. It only works as a recruiting line for workers if the cost of living is also lower. The data above says it isn't — at least not enough to compensate.

El Paso County workers earn 36% less than Denver County workers on average. Typical homes in El Paso cost only about 19% less than typical homes in Denver. The wage discount is steeper than the housing discount, by a lot. So the average El Paso worker covers 57.8% of the income needed to buy a typical home in their own metro. The average Denver worker covers 68.2%. Both are bad. El Paso's is worse, and the gap is widening.

This is the structural truth underneath the headlines about jobs landed and ribbons cut. Recruitment activity has been strong. Worker outcomes — measured by whether the recruited workforce can afford to live where they're being recruited to — have been deteriorating. Both things are true. The first is what gets celebrated. The second is what shows up at kitchen tables.

None of this is an indictment of the EDC, the Chamber, or the recruited companies. They're doing what they're structured to do. The question is whether the public agencies funding the recruitment apparatus — the city, the county, the utilities, the state — are measuring the right outcomes. Right now they measure jobs landed and capital invested. They don't measure whether the workers being recruited can afford to stay.

That's a fixable problem. It starts with the data being legible. Hence this dashboard.

Methodology Show your work

This dashboard exists because the comparison numbers it shows aren't published anywhere in one place. Every figure on this page comes from public, free, government or nonprofit datasets. Every series ID is documented. Every limitation is named.

Source 1 — BLS Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages

The federal QCEW dataset covers approximately 95% of U.S. employment. Free, public, updated quarterly. Series IDs used:

  • ENU0804140510 — El Paso County, all-industry private, average weekly wage
  • ENU0803140510 — Denver County, all-industry private, average weekly wage
  • ENU0804110510 — El Paso County, all-industry private, employment level
  • ENU0803110510 — Denver County, all-industry private, employment level

Limitations: QCEW reports average weekly wage, not median. Annualized by × 52. Doesn't capture benefits, equity, or non-wage compensation. Aerospace-specific (NAICS 3364) data isn't reliably exposed at the county level in the public API; the all-industry comparison is what's reported here.

Source 2 — Zillow Research

Zillow Research publishes county- and metro-level housing indices as free downloadable CSVs at zillow.com/research/data. Datasets used:

  • ZHVI All Homes (county level, smoothed, seasonally adjusted) — typical home value, 35th–65th percentile
  • New Homeowner Affordability Income Needed (metro level, 20% down) — Zillow's calculation of household income required to qualify at the standard 30% debt-to-income ratio at current mortgage rates

Limitations: ZHVI is "typical home value," not median sale price. Income Needed is published at the metro level — Colorado Springs MSA and Denver-Aurora-Lakewood MSA — used here as the closest proxy for the underlying counties. Zillow's mortgage-rate assumptions update with each release; the affordability spike in 2022 reflects both rising home values and rising rates.

Source 3 — Public Investment per Job

Chart 3 is built across three views. Each view captures a different layer of what's documented in OEDIT meeting minutes and Chamber & EDC announcements. The data below explains what's visible and what isn't.

What's documented and shown:

  • JGITC ceilings — the maximum credit a company can earn over 8 years, public via OEDIT EDC meeting minutes. This is a tax credit ceiling, not cash paid. Companies must create and maintain every committed job at or above county average annual wage to vest each year's portion.
  • Pikes Peak Enterprise Zone credits — partial; per-company amounts are sometimes published in Chamber announcements but aren't always visible.
  • Project Boron stack — the four-layer documented total ($25.83M) is visible in EDC minutes because the deal is large and the stack was disclosed during commission discussion. Most deals don't have this level of public disclosure.

What's still pending records requests:

  • Strategic Fund cash grants (the per-job cash incentive layered with JGITC, typically $2,500–$5,000 per qualifying job paid only after job verification)
  • Local Deal Closing Fund matches from the Chamber & EDC ($1,000–$5,000 per net new job)
  • County and city tax abatements (CORA pending to County Clerk and City Clerk)
  • Business Personal Property Tax forgone revenue — El Paso County is the only Colorado county that doesn't collect this; the implied subsidy per company is calculable from equipment investment but requires records request
  • EDC operating contributions per project (aggregate published; per-project breakdowns CORA pending)

Important caveat on JGITC: approvals are conditional on the company actually selecting Colorado. Companies don't always claim the full credit, and some are deferred or declined at the EDC level. The OEDIT Annual Reports include a "JGITC Tax Credit Certificates Issued" table showing what's actually delivered vs. approved. That distinction matters and will be added to a future version of this dashboard.

Why Project Boron matters as a reference: Boron is the cleanest documented example of a fully-stacked package in the recent El Paso County minutes. Most reporting and most public discussion focuses on JGITC alone because it's the most visible layer. For Boron, JGITC is 11% of the package; local cash is 43.7%. Future per-job calculations should account for the full stack, not just the headline number.

Update cadence

Quarterly. QCEW publishes on a quarterly lag, so re-running the data pull every three months captures the most recent fully-published period. Zillow updates monthly. Incentive data updates whenever new OEDIT minutes are published or when CORA responses arrive.

Reproducibility

The Python scripts that pull the data are published alongside this dashboard. bls_pull.py hits the BLS Open Data API. join_data_v2.py joins the BLS output with the Zillow CSVs and computes the affordability metrics. Anyone can re-run them. Anyone can fork them for another county. The methodology is meant to travel.

Accessibility Statement The thing the marketing department doesn't include

This dashboard targets WCAG 2.1 AA conformance. Specifically:

  • All charts have descriptive title and desc elements for screen reader users.
  • Every chart includes a hidden, expandable data table (the "Show data table" toggle) so the underlying numbers are accessible without relying on the SVG.
  • Color choices for chart series are tested for distinguishability in deuteranopia and protanopia — the El Paso brass and Denver deep-green pair survive most color-vision differences. Where color alone might be ambiguous, line endpoints are labeled directly.
  • Page heading hierarchy is clean and tested. Skip-to-content navigation, semantic landmarks, and keyboard focus order are honored.
  • Text contrast on body copy is well above the 4.5:1 minimum.
  • No JavaScript is required to read this page. Charts render as static SVG. Data tables render as semantic HTML.

Known limitations: this dashboard has not yet been audited with axe DevTools or by a screen reader user directly. If you find an accessibility issue, the contact for this project is in the footer below — please report it. Fixing it makes the dashboard better for everyone.
