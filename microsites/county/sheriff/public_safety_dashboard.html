    Data Gaps & Insights

    What the data doesn't show — and why it matters

    El Paso County residents deserve complete, accessible public safety data. This section documents what's missing, why it matters, and what can be done about it.

    Colorado Springs is the only major Colorado city where violent crime is increasing
    Between 2022 and 2025, Denver's violent crime rate fell 14%, Aurora's fell 36%, Fort Collins decreased. Colorado Springs rose 12% — the only large city in the state moving in the wrong direction, according to CBI data. Meanwhile, the data gaps below make it harder for residents to see the full picture.

    EPSO's official crime map shows zero EPSO data
    Critical
    El Paso County Sheriff's Office directs the public to communitycrimemap.com (LexisNexis) as its crime tool. That platform shows only CSPD data. EPSO patrols 2,130 square miles and responds to 153,000+ calls for service per year — none of which are publicly accessible in real time. Residents in Falcon, Peyton, Security-Widefield, and Black Forest have no access to real-time crime data for their communities.
    153,000+ calls/year · 0 accessible in real time 2,130 sq mi with no coverage
    Applicable law
    CRS 24-72-203 (CORA — right to inspect public records) · CRS 24-72-301 (CCJRA) · CRS 29-21-101 (model transparency act)
    Required action
    EPSO must publish a real-time incident data API — or clearly disclose that no data exists — rather than redirecting the public to a CSPD-only tool without disclosure.
    LexisNexis tool shows 93% fewer incidents than CSPD open data
    Critical
    EPSO's official crime tool showed 272 records in a 28-day window. CSPD open data showed approximately 3,585 incidents for the same period. The gap is structural: LexisNexis caps display at 500 records and shows only 9 of 28+ crime categories. This limitation is not disclosed to the public on any EPSO website.
    LexisNexis: 272 records · CSPD API: 3,585 · gap: 93%
    Applicable law
    CRS 24-72-204(3)(a)(I) — accuracy of public records disclosures
    EPSO ADA contact email bouncing — approx. 12 months
    Critical
    The email address listed as EPSO's ADA contact (shrcommunity@elpasoco.com) has been bouncing since approximately April 2025. A second mailto link on the same page misdirects to a recruiting inbox. The DOJ Title II web accessibility rule — requiring WCAG 2.1 AA compliance — took effect April 24, 2026.
    ADA email bouncing ~12 months DOJ deadline: April 24, 2026
    Applicable law
    28 C.F.R. § 35.107 · 42 U.S.C. § 12132 · 28 C.F.R. Part 35 Subpart H (WCAG 2.1 AA)
    Coroner homicide count doesn't match what public tools show
    High
    The El Paso County Coroner confirmed 51 homicides in 2024. EPSO's public tool (LexisNexis) annualizes to approximately 26. No public source reconciles these figures or explains the gap. The difference reflects both jurisdictional coverage (coroner covers all of El Paso County; CSPD covers only the city) and the structural 93% data gap in LexisNexis.
    Coroner 2024: 51 · LexisNexis annualized: ~26 · gap: ~25 homicides
    Recommended action
    EPSO and EPCCO should publish a joint annual homicide reconciliation: coroner-confirmed, CSPD-investigated, and EPSO-investigated counts in one publicly accessible document.
    Drug deaths and drug enforcement trend in opposite directions
    High
    EPSO's 2025 Annual Report shows drug crimes down 17%. The Coroner's 2024 data shows methamphetamine deaths up 7%, with 154 total overdose deaths — the leading cause of accidental death in the county. No public source reconciles these opposing trends or explains whether reduced enforcement is affecting overdose outcomes.
    EPSO drug enforcement: ▼17% · Meth deaths: ▲7% 154 overdose deaths — #1 cause of accidents
    CSPD data uses different counting rules than FBI/CBI — undisclosed publicly
    High
    The CSPD open data API carries a metadata disclaimer: "This dataset does not apply the same counting rules as official data reported to the Colorado Bureau of Investigations and the FBI." This disclaimer does not appear on the public-facing crime map page. The 511,000+ records cannot be compared to state or federal statistics without error — but most users don't know that.
    Recommended action
    The CSPD crime map page (policedata.coloradosprings.gov) must display the methodology disclaimer prominently so residents know these figures differ from official CBI/FBI annual statistics.
    Three municipalities have no accessible public crime data
    Medium
    Fountain (~34,000 residents), Manitou Springs (~6,000), and Monument (~10,000) each have their own police departments but publish no real-time crime data APIs. Approximately 50,000 El Paso County residents have zero public access to crime statistics for their city beyond annual CBI filings.
    Recommended action
    El Paso County should pursue a regional open data agreement requiring all agencies to publish to the existing Tyler Data & Insights platform at policedata.coloradosprings.gov.
    Coroner 2025 data not yet published — 9-12 month public lag
    Medium
    The most recent coroner data covers 2024, released September 2025. The 2025 homicide, suicide, and overdose statistics will not be available until approximately Q3 2026. No quarterly release exists. Residents currently have no public access to 2025 death statistics even though the year has been complete for four months.
    Recommended action
    EPCCO should publish quarterly mortality trend data (cause and manner totals only, no PII) at coroner.elpasoco.com to reduce the public information lag.

    What should happen next

    1
    EPSO must publish a real-time incident data API
    Extend the existing Tyler Data & Insights platform (policedata.coloradosprings.gov) to include EPSO incident data. The platform is already licensed and operational. Minimum fields: incident date, crime category, community/zip, patrol sector.
    CRS 24-72-203CRS 29-21-101
    2
    Restore EPSO's ADA contact and fix the blank crime page
    The ADA contact email must be restored immediately. The Crime Reports & Maps page must be populated with actual content — at minimum: CORA contact, description of available data, accurate LexisNexis disclosure. Both must happen before or by April 24, 2026.
    28 C.F.R. § 35.10742 U.S.C. § 12132
    3
    CSPD must add a methodology disclaimer to its public crime map
    A prominent banner on policedata.coloradosprings.gov must state that these figures use different counting rules than official CBI/FBI statistics. The Tyler platform allows this to be added in minutes as a dataset description — no engineering required.
    CRS 24-72-204(3)(a)(I)34 U.S.C. § 41303
    4
    EPSO, EPCCO, and Public Health should publish a joint drug trend report
    Drug enforcement trends (EPSO), overdose deaths (coroner), and treatment utilization (Public Health) should be published together annually. The current situation — where drug crimes are reported as falling while meth deaths are rising, with no reconciliation — actively misleads the public.
    CRS 29-21-101
    5
    Coroner should publish quarterly mortality totals
    A simple quarterly release of manner-of-death and cause-of-death totals (no personal information) would reduce the current 9-12 month public lag to under 3 months. This requires minimal staff effort and could be automated from existing systems.
    CRS 30-10-601
