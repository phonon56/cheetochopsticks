What do these crime categories mean?
⚠️ Against society
Offenses where society itself is the "victim" — no single individual is directly harmed.
Examples: drug possession/trafficking, weapons violations, prostitution, disorderly conduct, trespassing, liquor law violations, gambling offenses.
🏠 Against property
Offenses involving taking, damaging, or destroying someone's property. The victim is property, not a person directly.
Examples: burglary, larceny/theft, motor vehicle theft, arson, fraud, embezzlement, shoplifting, vandalism.
🚨 Against persons
Offenses directly targeting a person's safety, body, or freedom. A real individual is the victim in every case.
Examples: murder, assault (simple and aggravated), robbery, rape and sex offenses, kidnapping, human trafficking, intimidation.

These categories follow the FBI's National Incident-Based Reporting System (NIBRS) definitions. "Against society" is often the largest category because it includes drug-related calls, which are very common. High society crime in a ZIP does not necessarily mean it is more dangerous to walk around — it reflects enforcement activity and calls for service.

    Data Gaps & Insights

    What the data doesn't show — and why it matters

    El Paso County residents deserve complete, accessible public safety data. This section documents what's missing, why, and what can be done.

    Colorado Springs is the only major Colorado city where violent crime is increasing
    Between 2022 and 2025, Denver fell 14%, Aurora fell 36%, Fort Collins decreased. Colorado Springs rose 12% — the only large city in the state moving the wrong direction, per CBI data. The data gaps below make this harder for residents to see clearly.

    1. EPSO's official crime map shows zero EPSO data
    Critical
    EPSO directs residents to communitycrimemap.com (LexisNexis). That platform shows CSPD data only. EPSO patrols 2,130 sq mi with 153,000+ calls/year — none accessible in real time. Residents in Falcon, Peyton, Security-Widefield, and Black Forest have no access to real-time crime data for their area.
    153,000+ calls/yr · 0 in real time2,130 sq mi without coverage
    Applicable law
    CRS 24-72-203 (CORA) · CRS 24-72-301 (CCJRA) · CRS 29-21-101 (transparency act)
    2. LexisNexis tool shows 93% fewer incidents than CSPD open data
    Critical
    EPSO's official tool showed 272 records in a 28-day window. CSPD open data showed ~3,585 incidents for the same period. LexisNexis caps at 500 records and shows only 9 of 28+ crime categories. This limitation is not disclosed.
    LexisNexis: 272 · CSPD API: ~3,585 · gap: 93%
    Applicable law
    CRS 24-72-204(3)(a)(I) — accuracy of public record disclosures
    3. EPSO ADA contact email listed; crime reports page is blank
    Critical
    The Crime Reports & Maps page (epcsheriffsoffice.com/services/crime-reports-maps/) shows only a heading and site footer — verified April 12, 2026. The digital accessibility page contains two typos in headings: "Digital Accessibilty" and "Accomodation." The DOJ Title II WCAG 2.1 AA rule took effect April 24, 2026.
    Crime page: blank — verified Apr 12, 2026DOJ deadline: Apr 24, 2026
    Applicable law
    28 C.F.R. § 35.107 · 42 U.S.C. § 12132 · 28 C.F.R. Part 35 Subpart H
    4. CSPD data uses different counting rules than CBI/FBI — undisclosed on public page
    Critical
    The CSPD open data portal carries this disclaimer in its metadata: "This dataset provided by CSPD does not apply the same counting rules as official data reported to the Colorado Bureau of Investigations and the Federal Bureau of Investigation. This means comparisons to those datasets would be inaccurate." This does not appear on the public-facing crime map page.
    Required action
    Add a prominent banner on policedata.coloradosprings.gov citing the methodology difference. This is a text field on an existing platform — no engineering required.
    5. Coroner confirmed 51 homicides; public tools show ~26 annualized
    High
    EPCCO confirmed 51 county-wide homicides in 2024. LexisNexis annualizes to ~26. No public source reconciles these counts. The gap reflects jurisdictional coverage (coroner covers all of El Paso County) and the 93% LexisNexis data gap.
    Recommended action
    EPSO and EPCCO should publish a joint annual homicide reconciliation: coroner-confirmed, CSPD-investigated, and EPSO-investigated counts in one accessible document.
    6. Drug enforcement ▼17% while meth deaths ▲7% — no reconciliation published
    High
    EPSO 2025 Annual Report: drug crimes down 17%. Coroner 2024: methamphetamine deaths up 7%, 154 total overdose deaths — the #1 cause of accidental death. No public source addresses why these trend in opposite directions.
    EPSO drug enforcement: ▼17% · meth deaths: ▲7%
    7. Three municipalities — no accessible public crime data
    Medium
    Fountain (~34k residents), Manitou Springs (~6k), and Monument (~10k) have their own police departments but no real-time data APIs. ~50,000 El Paso County residents have zero access to real-time crime data for their city.
    Recommended action
    El Paso County should pursue a regional open data agreement using the existing Tyler Data & Insights platform at policedata.coloradosprings.gov.
    8. Coroner 2025 data not published — 9–12 month public lag
    Medium
    Most recent coroner data covers 2024. The 2025 homicide, suicide, and overdose statistics will not be public until ~Q3 2026. No quarterly release exists.
    Recommended action
    EPCCO should publish quarterly mortality totals (no PII) to reduce the public lag from 9–12 months to under 3.
    9. CSPD directs residents to a second crime feed that uses different data and different rules — with no reconciliation
    Critical
    The CSPD page "Crimes and Calls for Service in Your Neighborhood" (coloradosprings.gov) sends residents to myneighborhoodupdate.net, a Corona Solutions product showing CAD (dispatch) data — while the CSPD open data portal shows RMS (report) data. The two feeds measure different things: CAD includes non-crime calls but excludes sexual assaults; RMS includes all NIBRS-reportable offenses but excludes calls that closed without a report. Corona Solutions defines its own categories. Addresses are approximate, not real. No page on coloradosprings.gov tells residents which feed they are looking at or why the two disagree.
    2 feeds · 2 taxonomies · 0 public reconciliationSexual assault excluded from CAD feed
    Required action
    Add a single methodology page on coloradosprings.gov/police that explains: (a) what CAD data is, (b) what RMS data is, (c) which page shows which, (d) why the numbers differ, (e) what is excluded from each. This is a text deliverable — no engineering required.
    Applicable law
    CRS 24-72-204(3)(a)(I) — accuracy of public record disclosures · CCJRA 24-72-301 et seq.
    10. Corona Solutions / myneighborhoodupdate.net — third-party Title II ADA exposure
    Critical
    The tool CSPD links to as its neighborhood-level crime interface has multiple WCAG 2.1 Level A failures and no published accessibility conformance statement. Specifics: served over HTTP (not HTTPS); primary actions use javascript: pseudo-links with no accessible name/role; an interactive map with no keyboard-navigable or tabular alternative; charts rendered without text equivalents; the address input has no programmatic label visible in the markup; alt text on decorative icons is inconsistent or missing; the "About" page is two sentences with no VPAT link. The DOJ April 2024 final rule under ADA Title II covers third-party content used by a public entity to offer services to the public — the city cannot disclaim responsibility by pointing at the vendor. Colorado Springs' compliance deadline was April 24, 2026.
    WCAG 2.1 Level A failures: multipleNo HTTPSNo VPAT publishedDOJ deadline passed: Apr 24, 2026
    Required action
    Either (a) remove the link from the CSPD page until the vendor provides a conformant release with a published VPAT, (b) build an accessible front-end on top of the same CSPD CAD feed (Tyler Data & Insights already hosts CSPD's open data and can accept CAD), or (c) replace the tool. Option (b) is the lowest-cost path and eliminates the duplicate vendor spend.
    Applicable law
    28 C.F.R. Part 35 Subpart H · 42 U.S.C. § 12132 · 28 C.F.R. § 35.107
    11. The CSPD open data portal itself fails WCAG 2.1 Level A — including a broken accessibility link
    Critical
    policedata.coloradosprings.gov — the Tyler Data & Insights (Socrata) portal where CSPD publishes its RMS crime data — has structural accessibility failures in its own chrome, independent of any dataset view. The home page, the "About" story, and dataset pages all render as empty HTML shells that require JavaScript to hydrate. A user or assistive technology that does not fully execute JavaScript receives a page with no content. The footer's "Accessibility" link points to http://www.socrata.com/accessibility — which (a) is served over unencrypted HTTP, and (b) returns an empty response body. The actual Tyler accessibility statement exists at support.socrata.com/hc/en-us/articles/360019056614 but is not linked from the portal. Tyler's own accessibility statement acknowledges that "AJAX and JavaScript would interfere with the use of accessible technologies" and promises "alternate versions... made available via keyboard navigation skip links" — those alternate versions do not exist on the CSPD deployment. The VPAT is "available upon request" rather than published. This is the city's primary open-data transparency surface.
    Empty server-rendered HTML (WCAG 4.1.1)Skip link to nonexistent #content (WCAG 2.4.1)Accessibility footer link: HTTP, blank bodyVPAT on request only
    Required action
    At minimum: (1) fix the footer "Accessibility" link to point to the correct, HTTPS URL of Tyler's actual statement, (2) require Tyler to publish the VPAT on the portal rather than "upon request," (3) publish an accessibility page on coloradosprings.gov/police describing the Tyler portal's conformance posture and offering an accessible alternative format on request. Request the current Tyler VPAT for Data & Insights / Socrata directly from datainsights-support@tylertech.com and post it publicly. The city is the public entity; the procurement relationship with Tyler does not transfer the Title II obligation.
    Applicable law
    28 C.F.R. Part 35 Subpart H · WCAG 2.1 SC 2.4.1, 4.1.1, 4.1.2, 1.3.1 · 42 U.S.C. § 12132

    Recommendations

    1
    EPSO must publish a real-time incident data API
    Extend the existing Tyler Data & Insights platform to include EPSO incident data. It is already licensed and operational. Minimum fields: date, crime category, community/zip, patrol sector.
    CRS 24-72-203CRS 29-21-101
    2
    Fix the blank EPSO crime page before April 24, 2026
    The Crime Reports & Maps page must contain actual content: what data exists, how to request it, and an honest disclosure that no real-time Sheriff data is publicly accessible.
    42 U.S.C. § 1213228 C.F.R. § 35.107
    3
    Add a methodology disclaimer to the CSPD crime map
    A prominent note on policedata.coloradosprings.gov must explain that these figures use different counting rules than CBI/FBI statistics. The Tyler platform allows this as a dataset description field — no engineering required.
    CRS 24-72-204(3)(a)(I)
    4
    Publish a joint annual drug trend report
    EPSO, EPCCO, and El Paso County Public Health should correlate enforcement data, overdose deaths, and treatment utilization in one annual report. The current situation — enforcement down 17%, meth deaths up 7%, no explanation — actively misleads the public.
    CRS 29-21-101
    5
    EPCCO should publish quarterly mortality totals
    Simple quarterly release of manner-of-death totals (no personal information) would reduce the 9–12 month public lag to under 3 months. Can be automated from existing systems.
    CRS 30-10-601
    6
    Publish a single CAD vs. RMS methodology page on coloradosprings.gov/police
    Residents currently see two CSPD crime feeds that disagree, with no explanation. A single public page should document: what CAD and RMS are, which tool shows which, what is excluded from each, and why the totals differ. This eliminates one of the most persistent sources of public confusion about crime data in Colorado Springs — at zero engineering cost.
    CRS 24-72-204(3)(a)(I)CCJRA 24-72-301
    7
    Remediate or replace the Corona Solutions third-party tool
    myneighborhoodupdate.net fails multiple WCAG 2.1 Level A criteria, is served over HTTP rather than HTTPS, and has no published accessibility conformance statement. The April 24, 2026 DOJ Title II deadline covers third-party content used by public entities. The lowest-cost remediation is to expose CAD data through the existing Tyler Data & Insights platform that already hosts bc88-hemr — the same platform, an additional dataset, no new vendor.
    28 C.F.R. Part 35 Subpart H42 U.S.C. § 12132
    8
    Fix the CSPD open data portal's own accessibility posture
    The Tyler/Socrata portal where CSPD publishes its crime data has a broken accessibility link in its footer (HTTP, empty response) and no publicly-posted VPAT. The city should: (1) request and publish Tyler's current VPAT for the Data & Insights platform, (2) replace the broken footer link with the correct HTTPS statement URL, (3) publish a short accessibility notice on coloradosprings.gov/police describing the portal's conformance and how residents who cannot use the JS-dependent interface can request an accessible alternative format (e.g., CSV by email). All three steps are text-only changes requiring no vendor engineering.
    28 C.F.R. Part 35 Subpart H42 U.S.C. § 12132
