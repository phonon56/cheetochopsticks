Dear Commissioners,

I am a veteran and an El Paso County resident. I served honorably, and I chose to make this community my home after my service ended. I am writing about a structural problem that is hurting the active-duty service members coming up behind me, the veterans who have already settled here, and most especially the children of both.

What I have watched happen here

Colorado Springs is sold to employers and to the Department of Defense as a place where you can get highly-educated, security-cleared talent at well below coastal salaries. The Chamber & Economic Development Corporation's own published number is that the 5,000 aerospace, defense, manufacturing, and cybersecurity jobs it has announced since 2022 pay an average of $65,000 per year — and that this is above the county's overall average. A junior enlisted soldier with a family at Fort Carson lives on substantially less.

At the same time, the federal Department of Justice and the Colorado Attorney General have established in open litigation that rents in markets like ours have been set, for at least a decade, by an algorithm — RealPage's pricing software — that aggregated confidential data from competing landlords and recommended coordinated rent levels. Greystar, the largest apartment manager in the country, settled with a nine-state attorney general coalition including Colorado for $7 million in November 2025. RealPage itself settled with DOJ on November 24, 2025. Several named defendants operate properties in El Paso County. The settlements stop the practice going forward but do not refund a single dollar of inflated rent already extracted from the soldier, the veteran, the schoolteacher, or the cleared aerospace engineer who signed those leases.

The Basic Allowance for Housing my younger comrades receive is calculated annually from local market rents. When the algorithm raises market rents, BAH rises to chase them. When BAH rises, every landlord in the area treats the new BAH as the floor. The cycle has been running for years. The arithmetic does not work for a family of four on E-5 pay, and it has not worked for a long time. Children in this county are food-insecure not because their parents do not work hard but because the rent took the grocery money before the grocery money existed.

What the Board can do — within existing authority

I am not asking the Board to enact rent control. State law preempts that. I am asking the Board to do four things that are already within its authority, that align with the Chamber's own published housing policy supporting "cooperation and partnership… to bring more units to market for all income levels," and that cost approximately zero because the Land Development Code is already being rewritten by Clarion Associates under a DOLA Strong Communities grant.

Four narrow asks

  1. Amend the model service plan to require disclosure of algorithmic pricing software use and any named-defendant status in U.S. v. RealPage.
  2. Authorize voluntary rent-limit agreements under CRS 38-12-301(2)–(4), pegged to local BAH for designated units.
  3. Publish a transparency registry mapping properties to ownership, defendant status, and pricing-software certification. The information is already public; nobody assembles it.
  4. Initiate an intergovernmental data-sharing agreement with Fort Carson's Garrison Public Affairs Office for a community housing impact assessment.

Why I am writing

I took an oath. So did my friends. So did my battle buddies. So did every soldier, sailor, airman, marine, guardian, and coast guardsman currently stationed in this county. The country owes them — and their children — at least the dignity of knowing whether the apartment they are signing a lease on is owned by a defendant in an antitrust case. That is not a partisan ask. It is a transparency ask. The Board has the authority. The timing is now. I respectfully ask the Board to act.

Thank you for your service to our community, and for your time.
