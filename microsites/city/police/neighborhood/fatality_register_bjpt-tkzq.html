IIYear-by-Year Breakdown

The strongest finding in this table is what it doesn't show: fatalities have not changed meaningfully in ten years. Colorado Springs kills between 34 and 57 people in traffic crashes every year, and has done so through population growth from 459,000 to 495,000, through COVID, through the completion of major road expansions, and through the 2024 speed-limit changes. The noise around the mean is small. The one apparent 2025 spike is one row.

IIIThe Pedestrian / Bicycle Silence

This was a "collapse" in the parent audit. With the row-level data in hand, it is not a collapse. It is an absolute flatline, with a knowable start date, that has been unreported on the live public dataset for nearly five years.

Critical · VRU-1 — 1,780 days of reported zero

The last pedestrian-involved crash in the public dataset was on May 5, 2021 (accident 2021-00017105, 9400 Vista Del Pico Blvd). The last bicycle-involved crash was May 4, 2021 (accident 2021-00017075, Boulder & Bonfoy). From May 31, 2021 through April 15, 2026 — 1,780 consecutive days, 58.5 months, 4.87 years — the dataset reports zero pedestrian crashes and zero bicycle crashes across every patrol division.

Colorado Springs is a city of roughly 483,000 people. Thirty national safety studies, the Vision Zero framework, and peer-city Socrata dashboards all indicate that a metropolitan area this size ordinarily reports 80–200 pedestrian-involved crashes per year and 30–80 bicycle-involved crashes per year. The pre-cutoff years in this same dataset confirm the normal pattern: 218 pedestrian and 117 bicycle crashes in 2016, holding steady through 2020. That baseline then collapses to zero in a single month and stays there.

The motorcycle field continues to populate normally over the same period — 222 in 2022, 205 in 2023, 256 in 2024, 289 in 2025, 40 so far in 2026. Whatever broke in May 2021 broke specifically for the pedestrian_involved and bicycle_involved columns and nothing else.

The diagnostic question is no longer whether the pipeline is broken. It is where: is the form field itself gone, is it being captured but dropped in the ETL to Socrata, or is it being mapped to a renamed column that never got re-exposed? A city staffer with access to the records-management system can answer this in an hour. The public has waited 1,780 days.

IVThe DUI Coding Anomaly

Among the 457 fatal crashes over ten years, exactly nine are coded DUI, DWAI, DUID in the Driver Contributing Factor field. Two percent.

The Colorado Department of Transportation's annual fatality reports and the NHTSA Fatality and Injury Reporting System (FIRS) both place alcohol-impaired driving share of Colorado fatalities between 30% and 35% year over year. Applied to 457 fatal crashes, that baseline would predict roughly 135 to 160 alcohol-involved fatal crashes. The dataset records nine.

Warning · DUI-1 — Coding rate fifteen times below baseline

Possible explanations, in descending order of likelihood: (a) DUI determination comes from toxicology results that typically arrive days or weeks after the crash report form is filed, and CSPD's records-management system does not backfill the field; (b) officers are coding DUI under the more ambiguous "OTHER FACTOR" or "NO APPARENT FACTOR" buckets and the data never gets recoded; (c) DUI crashes are being handled in a parallel case-management system (the Criminal Justice Information System) and not linked back to the crash record. Any one of these would explain the gap, and the public dataset acknowledges none of them.

This matters because the dataset is actively used by researchers, journalists, and policy advocates as a source of Colorado Springs crash causation. A person drawing conclusions from this dataset about alcohol's role in local traffic fatalities will conclude that impaired driving is a rounding-error cause. That conclusion is almost certainly wrong.

VAt-Fault Age Outliers

Warning Ten crashes across the dataset list an at-fault person older than 100, with ages ranging from 105 to 132. None of them are in the fatal-crash subset; they are distributed across years 2018 to 2025. The pattern suggests the age field is occasionally collecting three-digit typos that should be 15–35 (e.g., 17 entered as 117, 24 entered as 124). A validation bound of 0–110 would catch all ten and flag data entry for review.

On the other end of the range, six fatal crashes record an at-fault-person age of 9 or younger. One of these (2016-00025274, Cache La Poudre & Knob Hill) shows a two-year-old pedestrian as the coded at-fault person. A two-year-old cannot meaningfully be "at fault"; the field semantics here are likely confused between "at-fault person" and "first-listed involved person," and the form should either prevent this or require an explicit override. The six young-age rows are clustered in crashes where the young person was a pedestrian or bicycle rider, which is the exact combination where the age/fault conflation is most likely to occur.
