    Children

    How many children are hurt in Colorado Springs each year — and who is responsible for helping?

    The question parents actually ask isn't "what's the crime rate." It's "are kids safe." That question crosses agencies: child abuse and neglect go through El Paso County DHS, injuries and crashes go through CSPD and hospitals, and the coroner picks up the most serious cases after the fact. CSPD's Data Hub does not have a children-specific section. Bringing the numbers together is the first reorganization this page performs.

    What the numbers actually say
    EPC leads Colorado in hotline calls — not because abuse is worst here, but because the community reports more consistently. Five military installations, a strong pediatric healthcare network, and active mandatory reporters (teachers, clergy, clinicians) produce volume. A hotline call is not a finding of abuse; it's a concern being raised. The movement from 16,100 calls to ~665 legally-involved cases is the system screening — most calls resolve with services, family support, or no action needed.

The CSPD Data Hub reports crime by city ZIP and never introduces the people themselves. This section does, because population is context, and context changes what the crime numbers mean.

    The honest description
    Colorado Springs is often called “a military city.” It is a diverse working city with an unusually large military presence. Healthcare is the largest single employer. Tech and aerospace are the fastest-growing. Retail and service jobs employ more residents than the military. The military is a defining feature, not the whole picture. When you read CSPD crime data against city demographics, you are reading it against nurses, contractors, teachers, Airmen, restaurant workers, retirees, and families — in that order of numerical weight.

    Real local household data — not yet published for public use
    The El Paso County Assessor has the actual parcel and dwelling-unit data, by address. The city's Planning Department has building-permit records, by neighborhood.     What to request
    A public housing dataset from the El Paso County Assessor ↗ and city Planning Department: parcels, dwelling units, housing type, year built, by neighborhood. The data exists in their systems; the public view is missing. Until then, this page cites Census ACS estimates and flags them as such.
    What this says about neighborhoods, before any crime number
    A neighborhood's character — family-heavy, senior-heavy, renter-heavy, newly-built versus established — changes what counts as normal. Downtown (80903) has ~16,500 residents but tens of thousands of daytime workers, so its crime rate per resident looks extreme while its crime rate per person-actually-there looks ordinary. Briargate has a young-family profile that reads as “quiet” in the numbers because the numbers don't know it's a young-family profile. A high-renter ZIP may show more noise and property calls without that being a sign of danger — renters move more often, call more often, and have less neighborhood social cohesion by default. Population is context. Crime without context is just noise.

    What these categories tell you
    About two-thirds of CSPD's budget is Patrol and Investigations — uniformed response and detectives. The rest supports them: training new officers, processing evidence, managing records, running background systems, and handling the specialized programs (body cameras, homeless outreach, community relations, internal affairs). Community-facing programs like PLAY COS and the Homeless Outreach Team are small fractions of the budget but highly visible. The largest civilian-support cost is logistics — fleet, buildings, and technology — which keep the department operating.

    Trust & accountability

    If I have a problem, who do I call — and how do I report an officer if I need to?

    The most overlooked resident question, and the most important. Most people will never need to report an officer. But they deserve to know the channel exists, who receives the complaint, what happens next, and how retaliation is prevented. Colorado Springs is a deeply military city. Service members already know this system — the military has had a structured, protected, escalatable grievance process for decades. Civilian policing can learn from that standard.

    What happens after you file — the five outcomes
    CSPD classifies every completed complaint into one of five findings: Sustained (misconduct did occur; discipline follows), Not Sustained (cannot be proved or disproved), Unfounded (facts show misconduct did not occur), Exonerated (the act happened and was justified and legal), or Policy Failure (the act was within policy but the policy should change). Sustained cases carry eight possible discipline levels, from documented counseling to termination. The aggregate counts by finding category are not published — they are collected internally.

    How the military does it — Article 138, UCMJ, and the IG

    Colorado Springs has five military installations. Many residents know this system already.
    Benchmark

    The military is not a model of perfection on every front, but on one specific thing — how a service member raises a grievance against a commanding officer — the system is more structured, more published, more protected from retaliation, and more auditable than what most civilian police departments offer. Comparing the two makes clear where the civilian gap actually is.

    Military — Article 138 UCMJ + IG

    Fort Carson · Peterson · Schriever · USAFA · Cheyenne Mountain
    • Written pathway. Service member first files a written request for redress with the commander. The commander has 15 days to respond.
    • Escalation guaranteed. If denied, service member files a formal Article 138 complaint, which must be forwarded up to the general court-martial convening authority (GCMCA).
    • Finding is investigated. GCMCA is required to investigate and take action. The outcome becomes part of the command record.
    • Retaliation is prohibited by law. Commanders cannot restrict filings or retaliate. Retaliation has its own complaint channel (IG + DoD Hotline).
    • Separate IG channel. Inspector General handles broader issues — violations of law, policy, or ethics — including systemic problems and whistleblower protections.
    • Timeline is published. 90-day filing window (180 for Air Force). Each branch publishes its full procedure (AR 27-10 for Army, AFI 51-904 for Air Force, etc.).
    • Resources are free. Judge Advocate General (JAG) attorneys provide guidance at no cost to the service member.

    CSPD — Internal Affairs

    Professional Standards Division · 705 S Nevada Ave
    • Intake channels exist. Phone, email, in-person, online, mail, anonymous. Good — the front door is open.
    • No published response timeline. No equivalent to the military's 15-day window or 90-day filing cap. Investigations "take as long as they take."
    • No required escalation. If you are dissatisfied with the IA finding, there is no external review body in Colorado Springs. Denver and Boulder have independent monitors; Colorado Springs does not.
    • Five outcome categories exist — but aggregate counts by category, by year, by division, are not published.
    • No public sustain rate. A resident cannot see how often IA sustains complaints versus finds them unfounded. This is the single number that tells the public whether the process works.
    • Procedure is partly published on PowerDMS (public.powerdms.com/CSPD2). The front-door complaint instructions are on coloradosprings.gov, but the full investigation, discipline, and appeal procedure is in scattered SOP documents.
    • Retaliation protection is implied but not statutory. No equivalent to 10 U.S.C. § 938.
    The point of this comparison
    Many Colorado Springs residents — active-duty, retired, spouses, adult children of service members — already know Article 138 and the IG system. They bring that expectation to civilian life. When the civilian equivalent is less transparent, slower, and has no external review, the gap is not "civilians don't trust police" — it's "civilians used to a working system notice when a system is weaker." CSPD is not uniquely bad here; most civilian police departments in the US fall short of the military standard on this specific dimension. Colorado Springs, with the military density it has, is unusually well positioned to lead the fix.
    Aggregate complaint data + an independent review channel
    Two things would close most of the gap: (1) publish an annual aggregate report — complaints filed, investigation outcomes by category, discipline imposed, median time to close — and (2) create an independent review channel so residents dissatisfied with IA findings have a meaningful escalation. Denver and Boulder have adopted this model; Colorado Springs has not. The aggregate data already exists in the Early Intervention Program that CSPD monitors internally. Publication is a format decision, not a collection decision.
    What to request
    (1) An annual aggregate IA report on the CSPD Data Hub. (2) A City Council-led review of civilian oversight models in peer Colorado cities (Denver, Boulder, Fort Collins) to determine whether Colorado Springs would benefit from a similar structure.

    Shootings in context

    Officer-involved shootings — in the context of all shootings in the region

    CSPD publishes officer-involved shootings as a separate story, which makes OIS feel like its own category of public-safety concern. The honest framing is different. OIS is one slice of a much larger picture of firearm violence in El Paso County — a slice that draws specific oversight attention, but that is numerically dwarfed by firearm suicides and by homicides involving firearms not fired by officers. Both contexts matter. Neither should be looked at alone.

    Not every firearm homicide in the county involves CSPD jurisdiction. Not every OIS results in a death. This is an order-of-magnitude view, not a precise overlap. The point: OIS accounts for roughly 2–5% of firearm-related deaths in El Paso County in a typical year. It draws outsize attention because of the oversight questions it raises, not because of its share of the total.

    Both contexts are correct, and both are needed
    OIS is rightly held to a higher scrutiny standard than civilian shootings — officers carry state authority and taxpayer-funded weapons. Publishing OIS as its own story acknowledges that. But when the OIS story sits on a page with no link to the larger shooting picture, residents can walk away thinking police shootings are the city's main firearm problem. They are not. Firearm suicide is roughly 20× more common. Telling both truths, in the same place, is how honest public-safety reporting works.

    Follow-through matters

    Published in CSPD datasets

    What you can find on the Data Hub
    • Year of incident
    • Number of shootings per year
    • Primary basis (CDCJ category — e.g., suspect armed, suspect fleeing)
    • Subject armed: yes / no / unknown
    • Type of weapon involved
    • Whether the shooting was fatal
    • Subject demographics (on CDCJ state reporting)

    Not published anywhere

    What residents actually need to know — follow-through
    • District Attorney's ruling (justified / not justified / pending)
    • Date of DA decision, and time-to-decision
    • Whether the involved officer was disciplined, and how
    • Whether the involved officer is still employed by CSPD
    • Whether a civil suit was filed
    • Civil settlement amount (if any)
    • Whether the officer had prior force complaints on record
    • Independent monitor / review panel findings (if any existed)
    Why follow-through matters — to everyone
    The silence on outcomes helps no one. Shootings that were cleared by the DA look identical in the public record to ones that were not. Officers with clean findings get tarred alongside officers who were disciplined. Families of those killed don't know whether the system took the case seriously. Residents cannot distinguish between "handled well" and "buried." The published data is a list of events. Residents need the consequences.
    Outcome register for every officer-involved shooting since 2010
    A single Socrata dataset with one row per OIS and columns for: DA finding, date of finding, officer discipline, officer employment status, civil suit filed (Y/N), settlement amount, review outcome. The 4th Judicial District Attorney reviews every OIS in El Paso County — the data exists. What's missing is the publication step and the cross-agency agreement to link DA outcomes to CSPD incident records.
    What to request
    An MOU between CSPD and the 4th Judicial DA to publish a joint annual OIS outcome register, maintained on the CSPD Data Hub using the existing Tyler/Socrata platform.

    accessibility audit

    Six story pages. Six empty HTML shells. One broken accessibility link.

    Every story page on policedata.coloradosprings.gov returns the same HTML: a title, the portal chrome, zero content. The substance loads via JavaScript after the browser parses the markup. A screen reader, a text browser, a slow connection, or a JavaScript-disabled environment sees a blank page with the right title. Under the DOJ April 2024 Title II rule — effective April 24, 2026 for Colorado Springs — this is a Level A structural failure, on the city's primary open-data transparency surface.

    Empty server-rendered HTML on all 6 story URLs

    Arrests, Officer-Involved Shootings, Demographics of Sworn Officers, Crashes, Crimes Against Persons/Property/Society, and the portal home — every one returns the portal chrome and nothing else. Content appears only after JS executes. No noscript fallback.

    WCAG 2.1 SC 4.1.1, 4.1.2, 1.3.1

    Skip link targets #content — which doesn't exist in the DOM

    Every story includes <a href="#content">Skip to main content</a>. The #content element is never rendered in the server-sent HTML. The skip link fails silently for AT users.

    WCAG 2.1 SC 2.4.1 Bypass Blocks (Level A)

    "Accessibility" footer link: HTTP, empty response

    The footer on every page of the portal links to http://www.socrata.com/accessibility. HTTPS is not offered. The response body is empty. The actual Tyler statement lives at support.socrata.com/hc/en-us/articles/360019056614 — not linked from anywhere on the portal.

    WCAG 2.4.4 · Principle: trust & security

    /browse blocked by robots.txt

    The dataset browse page — the only way to discover what datasets exist — is blocked from search engines and automated accessibility tooling. Manual navigation through the JS interface is the only entry path.

    WCAG 2.4.5 Multiple Ways (Level AA)

    VPAT available only "upon request"

    Tyler's own statement says VPAT "assessment documents are available upon request." Under the 2024 DOJ rule, a public-entity deployment of a third-party platform should have a VPAT publicly posted. The city can request Tyler's current VPAT at datainsights-support@tylertech.com and publish it themselves.

    Best practice · DOJ rule preamble

    Tyler itself acknowledges its JS interferes with AT

    Tyler's statement: "For pages where the use of AJAX and JavaScript would interfere with the use of accessible technologies, alternate versions of those pages are provided." Those alternate versions do not exist on the CSPD deployment.

    How to read this honestly
    City population includes children, who cannot be sworn officers. The meaningful labor-pool comparison is the regional working-age adult population, which the CSPD story page doesn't show. Both adjustments reduce the apparent ratio differences, but don't eliminate them for women or for some underrepresented groups. This is context, not verdict. A ratio gap is a hiring / retention dataset, not a claim about individual officers or about enforcement. If and when the underlying data (stop outcomes, sustained-complaint rates, promotion pipelines) shows a problem attributable to demographics, the problem belongs to that specific dataset — not to this chart.
    Officer demographics by rank, hiring year, and attrition — time series, not snapshot
    The single snapshot above is the entire Demographics of Sworn Officers story on the CSPD Data Hub. Missing: how the composition shifts by rank (patrol → sergeant → lieutenant → commander), how it has moved over the last 5 and 10 years, and what the hiring and attrition trend says about direction. The HR data already tracks all three. The snapshot hides the trend.
    What to request
    Replace the snapshot with a time series and a rank breakdown. Both can be generated from existing HR data without collecting anything new.
    About this reorganization

    This page is a companion to the Public Safety Dashboard at public_safety_dashboard.html. It reframes the CSPD Data Hub stories around questions residents actually ask — children, neighborhood, taxes, overall crime, who to call and trust — and contextualizes officer-involved shootings against regional firearm numbers. Demographics are reported as context in an appendix, not as a lead frame.

    Where data is labeled "approximate" or "illustrative," the underlying join has not been published by CSPD and figures are order-of-magnitude from related public reports (Census ACS, EPCCO Annual Report, EPC Kids Count, 2024 Police Budget, city budget books).
