The Pattern Hidden in Plain Sight

These two datasets — crash locations and highway finance — are telling the same story from opposite ends. Crashes are becoming more severe even as total counts decline. Serious injuries nearly doubled between 2020 and 2024. Meanwhile the city is spending 5.6× more on road expansion than on maintenance of existing roads, funded by a sales tax base that contracts in recessions.

The rear-end crash dominance (20,259 crashes — 31% of all incidents) is a direct signature of high-speed arterials with frequent stops. Those are exactly the corridors being widened. Wider roads at higher speeds produce more severe rear-ends, not fewer. The Safety Action Plan identifies this. The finance data shows spending isn't yet following the diagnosis.

Federal Transit Admin received $7.34M — 17% of total revenue. But crash patterns show no corresponding shift toward safer, multi-modal infrastructure. That gap is the question the Safety Action Plan should be answering.

What the Data Is Actually Saying

Three observations · One economic connection
📱

Cell phones are likely the core issue

88% of crashes on dry roads. 70% in daylight. Low impairment. Rear-end dominant. Serious injuries rising as total crashes fall — meaning crashes are hitting harder, not more often. That pattern doesn't fit road design. It fits distracted driving at speed on clear roads in good light.

🚦

Small fixes matter — signs, signals, potholes

Signal timing that catches drivers off guard. Potholes that cause sudden swerves. Faded lane markings. Poorly lit intersections. These aren't headline projects — they're $500 fixes buried in a preservation budget that gets 15 cents of every road dollar.

🏪

Safe, maintained corridors grow small businesses

Road funding here runs 41% on local sales tax. Corridors where people feel safe stopping support the kind of foot traffic that small businesses need. More small businesses mean more local sales tax. The loop closes.

The case in one paragraph: Colorado Springs crashes at a below-average rate for its population size — the roads aren't the primary danger. What's rising is severity per crash, which tracks with distracted driving at speed on dry, clear roads. The most cost-effective response isn't more lane miles — it's keeping existing signals timed correctly, lane markings visible, and surfaces smooth enough that a distracted driver who looks up at the last second has a chance to brake. Infrastructure maintenance and small business growth are the same investment, approached from opposite ends.

Preservation wins — national norm
Plain English

For every $1 spent building or widening roads, the national average local government spends $5 maintaining and repairing roads that already exist — fixing potholes, repaving worn surfaces, repairing bridges.

Think of it like a house

The typical American city acts like a homeowner who spends $5 on fixing the plumbing, roof, and foundation for every $1 they spend building a new room. Keep what you have in good shape first.

What This Means in Plain Terms

Three things to know
✅
The roads aren't killing people at an above-average rate
Colorado Springs' fatality rate per 100,000 residents (~8.6) is about 30% below the national average of 12.21. By the most important measure — how many people die — the city is doing better than most of America.
⚠️
But crashes are getting more violent, not less
The serious injury rate per 1,000 crashes went from 20.2 in 2017 to 53.4 in 2024 — a 164% increase while the national rate stayed flat. Fewer total crashes, but each one is more likely to seriously hurt someone.
🏗️
The spending pattern is building a future problem
The 5.6:1 expansion-to-preservation ratio is the inverse of the national norm. Every road that gets widened today will need maintenance in 10–15 years. With road funding 41% dependent on sales tax, a recession could create a serious maintenance funding gap.
