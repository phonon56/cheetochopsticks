Revenue & economics

What the program generates — and what's not shown

Colorado Springs charges a $75 fine per citation, a non-moving infraction with no points on the driver's license. Here's what that translates to financially.

2024 gross revenue

$3.52M

At $75 per citation × 46,992 citations. The city's 2022 figure was $2.4M; 2026 is pacing to exceed $3.4M if Q1 holds.

Revenue per event captured

$26

Effective rate across all 134,103 events reviewed by CSPD in 2024. Two of three events are dismissed before becoming citations.

Foregone revenue, 2024

$6.53M

Dismissed events that could have been citations at $75 each. The officer review process rejects them as unreadable, exempt, or unclear.

Program vendor

Verra Mobility

Runs 300+ camera programs nationally. NYC pays them $998M over 5 years. CSPD's contract terms and fee structure are not publicly disclosed.

Monthly gross revenue

2024 2025 2026 (Q1 only)

Revenue peaks every year in late summer and early fall — likely a mix of higher traffic volumes, longer daylight hours lengthening amber-light exposure, and tourism. The October 2024 peak of $366,375 was the highest single month recorded.

What's not in the $75

The $75 is the base fine only. Actual revenue to the city is higher, and actual cost to residents who are cited can be higher still. The city charges:

  • Online and phone payment surcharges — convenience fees, amount not published on the program page
  • Late penalties if the citation isn't paid on time
  • Personal service fees if a citation is ignored and has to be served in person (roughly $29 under Denver's model)
  • Collections costs if the citation goes to collections

What the city's share actually is

CSPD states the program is "fully funded through fines" and that surplus revenue goes to the city's general fund (not CSPD's budget).

But the actual split between the city and Verra Mobility isn't public. In other cities, Verra charges either flat monthly per-camera fees (~$5,000–7,000/month typical) or a per-citation share. At 20 cameras, that's $1.2M–$1.7M/year off the top.

To know the real program margin, file a CORA request for the Verra Mobility contract and annual program financials.

Context

About this data

What the numbers mean, where they come from, and why some months look strange.

Violations vs. citations

A violation event is any time a camera captures a vehicle that may have run a red light. CSPD officers review each event before a citation is issued.

Roughly one in three events becomes a citation. The rest are dismissed — often because the plate is unreadable, the driver had a legitimate reason (emergency vehicle, funeral procession), or the footage doesn't clearly show a violation.

Why some months are blank or zero

Dashes mean the camera was offline or not yet installed. Several intersections came online mid-2022, so their 2022 numbers only reflect the months they were active.

Zeros in 2024 at S Academy/Airport and several months in 2023 reflect real outages. June 2023 saw a citywide drop to roughly 15% of normal — likely a system-wide processing issue, not a sudden drop in red-light running.

Lake Ave / E Cheyenne Mtn went offline in late November 2025 and came back online in February 2026, which is why its 2025 total ends low and its 2026 January shows a dash.

Data caveats

The city's own note describes this as "preliminary, moment-in-time data" from the vendor. Numbers can change as events continue to be reviewed.

Some totals on the original page don't match the sum of their monthly values. Totals shown here are computed from the monthly figures, which are the underlying data of record.
