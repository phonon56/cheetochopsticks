The trade-off hiding in the crash data

Colorado Springs has 20 red-light cameras generating about 47,000 citations a year. The cameras do reduce side-impact (T-bone) crashes. But they trade those crashes for a different type — rear-end collisions caused by drivers braking hard to avoid the $75 fine. Here's what the city's own data shows.

The pattern isn't unique to Colorado Springs. The Federal Highway Administration has documented that red-light cameras reduce right-angle crashes but increase rear-end collisions by about 15%. When arterials get wider and speeds go up, rear-ends at those speeds stop being fender-benders and start causing serious injuries. That's the exact signature showing up in the 2020→2024 severity trend here.

02

What traffic engineers already know

The engineering fix for red-light running is older than the cameras. It's called the yellow-change interval — how long the light stays yellow before turning red. Published research across multiple decades shows that extending the yellow interval reduces red-light running dramatically, without the rear-end trade-off cameras introduce.

Three findings worth knowing when you write your letter

The research literature is clear: engineering the intersection should come before enforcement. When Philadelphia extended the yellow interval at two intersections by one second, red-light violations fell 36% — before any camera was added. Cameras added later produced additional reductions on top of that.

Why this matters for your letter. When you write to the city, you're not asking for a policy change. You're asking them to verify that the yellow-change intervals at your intersection match what the ITE formula recommends for the actual approach speed. That's an engineering audit, not a political argument. It's harder for the city to refuse, because the benchmark is their own profession's standard.

How to read the flags. "Review" means the data shows something worth asking about — high volume, suspicious patterns, or a trend that deserves explanation. "Outage" means the camera has had multi-month gaps in reporting. "Declining" and "stable" mean the intersection is behaving normally. None of these flags are about whether your specific citation was valid; they're about whether the intersection as a whole is worth a signal-timing audit.

Three letters that actually move the needle

You don't need to wait for a public meeting or learn traffic engineering to participate. These three letters go to three different pressure points — the engineer who sets the signal timing, the insurer who pays out on the rear-end claims, and the elected official who answers to voters. Fill in your details, copy, send. Takes about 10 minutes.

Highlighted fields in amber are where you add your own information — intersection, policy number, district, and so on.

1
To · City of Colorado Springs

Request a yellow-interval timing audit

Why this matters. The Colorado Springs Traffic Engineering Division sets the yellow-change intervals at every signalized intersection, including all 20 with cameras. Colorado state law doesn't require them to publish these timings. A formal request puts the intervals on record against the ITE standard — which, if the intervals are short, creates a liability paper trail for the city.
Subject: Request for yellow-change interval review at [INTERSECTION NAME] Dear Colorado Springs Traffic Engineering Division, I am writing to formally request a review of the yellow-change interval duration at the signalized intersection of [INTERSECTION NAME], which is part of the City's red-light safety camera program. Specifically, I am requesting the following information under the Colorado Open Records Act: 1. The current yellow-change interval duration (in seconds) for each signal phase at this intersection. 2. The all-red clearance interval duration for each phase. 3. The 85th-percentile approach speed that was used to calculate these intervals. 4. The date of the most recent review of these timings. 5. Whether the current intervals meet the durations recommended by the Institute of Transportation Engineers' Extended Kinematic Equation (ITE 2020) for the actual approach speeds and intersection geometry. My concern is grounded in published research. The Federal Highway Administration recognizes appropriate yellow-interval timing as a proven safety countermeasure. A peer-reviewed 2008 field study in Philadelphia found that extending the yellow interval by one second reduced red-light violations by 36%. Texas A&M Transportation Institute research similarly found that a 0.5 to 1.5 second extension reduces red-light running by at least 50%, with drivers adapting but the benefit remaining durable. Colorado Springs crash data for 2017–2025 shows 20,259 rear-end collisions — 31% of all crashes in the city — compared to 10,978 T-bone crashes. Serious injuries have risen 75% from 2020 to 2024, even as total crash counts have declined. This pattern is consistent with inadequate yellow timing at high-speed arterials, where drivers brake hard to avoid photo citations and are struck from behind. I am not asking for a policy change. I am asking whether this intersection's yellow timing currently matches the engineering standards the city's own profession publishes. If it does, I would appreciate confirmation. If it does not, I would appreciate learning when the timing will be brought into conformance. I understand CORA requires a response within three business days, with extensions available if needed. Please reply to: [YOUR NAME] [YOUR ADDRESS] [YOUR EMAIL] [YOUR PHONE] Thank you for your time and for your work on traffic safety in our city. Sincerely, [YOUR NAME]

2
To · Your auto insurance company

Ask your insurer to advocate for proper signal timing

Why this is the most leveraged letter. Your insurer pays out on rear-end claims. They have actuaries, lobbyists, and lawyers. They already fund the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety — the industry's own research arm — which has been publishing on yellow-light timing for decades. Insurance companies carry more weight with city engineering departments than any individual resident. This letter flips the economic incentive: every rear-end caused by inadequate yellow timing is their payout, not yours.
Subject: Signal timing policy affecting rear-end claim frequency in Colorado Springs Dear Claims and Advocacy Team, I am a policyholder with [YOUR INSURANCE COMPANY], policy number [YOUR POLICY NUMBER]. I am writing about a systemic traffic-engineering issue in Colorado Springs, Colorado, that appears to be driving up rear-end crash frequency across the city — and therefore the claims your company pays out. The City of Colorado Springs operates 20 red-light safety cameras through a vendor contract with Verra Mobility. Per the city's own published crash data for 2017–2025, rear-end collisions now make up 31% of all crashes in the city — 20,259 incidents — outnumbering T-bone crashes by nearly two to one. Serious injuries rose 75% between 2020 and 2024 while total crash counts declined, indicating that the crashes that do happen are becoming significantly more severe. The peer-reviewed research on this is well established. Extending the yellow-change interval by one second reduces red-light violations by 36% (Retting et al., 2008) and reduces red-light running by 50% or more (Bonneson & Zimmerman, 2004). The Insurance Institute for Highway Safety — supported by your industry — has documented this effect. The Federal Highway Administration lists appropriate yellow timing as a proven safety countermeasure. Despite this, Colorado Springs does not publish the current yellow-change interval durations at its camera intersections, and the signal timings are not on record against the ITE Extended Kinematic Equation standard. This means residents have no public way to verify whether yellow timing matches engineering best practice — and insurers paying out on rear-end claims have no way to confirm that the intersections where claims are concentrated meet current standards. I am asking your company to do two things: 1. Review your rear-end claim frequency at the following Colorado Springs intersections: Platte & Chelton, Campus & Woodmen, Lake Ave & Cheyenne Mountain, Woodmen & Black Forest, W. Colorado & 31st St, and E. Platte & Union. These are the top-volume red-light camera intersections. 2. Use your company's established government-affairs channels to formally request that the Colorado Springs Traffic Engineering Division publish current yellow-change intervals and confirm they meet the ITE 2020 standard. Insurance industry advocacy on this issue has historically been effective in other jurisdictions. I am a policyholder who wants to avoid rear-end crashes, not be compensated for them after the fact. I would appreciate a reply indicating what steps your company will take. [YOUR NAME] [YOUR ADDRESS] [YOUR EMAIL] [YOUR PHONE] Policy #: [YOUR POLICY NUMBER] Thank you for your attention to a preventable problem. Sincerely, [YOUR NAME]

To · Your Colorado Springs City Council District Representative

Ask for a briefing on the signal-timing review

Why this one closes the loop. Your City Council rep can formally request a briefing from Traffic Engineering and the Municipal Security Unit. A council request gets a response with named staff, a timeline, and a paper trail. An individual CORA request might sit in the queue. A council request rarely does. Find your district at coloradosprings.gov/city-council.
Subject: Request for a briefing — yellow-interval timing at red-light camera intersections Dear Councilmember [COUNCILMEMBER NAME], I am a resident of District [YOUR DISTRICT NUMBER] writing to request that your office formally ask Traffic Engineering and the Municipal Security Unit for a briefing on the yellow-change interval timings at the city's 20 red-light camera intersections. Here is why this matters for our district specifically. The city's published crash data shows that rear-end collisions make up 31% of all Colorado Springs crashes — nearly twice the T-bone rate — and serious injuries have risen 75% between 2020 and 2024 even as total crashes declined. This pattern is the documented signature of inadequate yellow-interval timing at high-speed arterials, where drivers brake hard to avoid the $75 camera fine and are struck from behind. The peer-reviewed research is well established. Extending the yellow interval by one second reduces red-light violations by 36% on its own (Retting et al., 2008). The Federal Highway Administration recognizes yellow-interval timing as a proven safety countermeasure. The Institute of Transportation Engineers publishes the standard calculation (the Extended Kinematic Equation, 2020). Despite this, the current yellow-change intervals at Colorado Springs camera intersections are not publicly available and have not, to my knowledge, been confirmed against the ITE standard. I am asking three specific things: 1. Request from Traffic Engineering the current yellow-change interval (in seconds) at each of the 20 camera intersections, along with the 85th-percentile approach speed used to calculate each. 2. Request confirmation from Traffic Engineering as to whether those intervals meet or exceed the ITE Extended Kinematic Equation standard for the actual approach geometry. 3. If they do not meet the standard, request a timeline for bringing them into conformance, and an estimate of how many rear-end crashes per year this would likely prevent. This is an engineering audit, not a political debate. The outcome is either "yes, the timings meet the standard" — which is reassuring and informative — or "no, they don't, and here's the plan to fix it" — which is a direct safety win for our district. I would appreciate a reply with whatever steps your office is willing to take. I'd also welcome the opportunity to speak with your staff if it would help. [YOUR NAME] [YOUR ADDRESS, DISTRICT ##] [YOUR EMAIL] [YOUR PHONE] Thank you for representing us. Sincerely, [YOUR NAME]
